\subsection*{EC519 Project Emotion Recognition in Speech \hfill \normalfont\normalsize Che Yu}

Our goal is to recognise the emotion expressed in a short speech recording.
We compare three classical signal-processing feature representations for
speech, MFCC, LPC, and LPCC, all fed into the same hidden Markov model (HMM)
classifier so that the comparison isolates the effect of the feature choice
rather than the model. We used RAVDESS speech dataset sampled at $f_s = 16$\,kHz, 
classified into five emotions \emph{neutral, happy, sad, angry, fearful}, with a
speaker-independent train/test split so the model never sees a test speaker
during training.

\subsection*{General Pipeline}

Every feature set follows the same flow: the raw waveform is cut and windowed
into short overlapping frames, each frame is converted into a feature vector. 
The frame features are then turned into a sequence of discrete symbols, and an 
HMM is trained to predict the five emotion classes and model those sequences. 
Our pipeline is shown below:
\begin{equation*}
    \text{audio} \;\longrightarrow\;
    \text{framing + windowing} \;\longrightarrow\;
    \text{feature extraction} \;\longrightarrow\;
    \text{VQ symbols} \;\longrightarrow\;
    \text{HMM per class}.
\end{equation*}
\paragraph{(a) Preprocessing: framing and windowing.}
Speech is non-stationary on the scale of phonemes (50--200\,ms), but
\emph{quasi-stationary} inside short windows of about 25\,ms. Since we are 
not modeling phonetic detail, we can use this stationarity assumption to
slice the waveform into frames of $L_w = 25$\,ms with a hop of
$L_h = 10$\,ms (so consecutive frames overlap by 60\%). At 16\,kHz this
gives frames of $N = 400$ samples spaced by $H = 160$ samples. Then each frame 
is multiplied by a Hamming window
\(
    w[n] = 0.54 - 0.46\cos\!\bigl(2\pi n/(N-1)\bigr),
\)
which tapers the edges so the spectrum is not contaminated by the abrupt
discontinuities at the frame boundaries.
\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\linewidth]{../plots/hamming_window.png}
    \caption*{Hamming window ($N = 400$ samples = 25\,ms at 16\,kHz).}
\end{figure}
\paragraph{(b) Feature extraction.}
Each extractor turns a windowed frame into a fixed-length feature vector.
The three feature families capture the speech signal in different ways:

\emph{MFCC (Mel-Frequency Cepstral Coefficients).} A perceptually motivated
representation of the short-time spectral envelope. The frame is sent through
the following pipeline:
\begin{equation*}
    s_t^{w}[n] \;\longrightarrow\;
    |\mathrm{FFT}|^{2} \;\longrightarrow\;
    \text{mel filterbank} \;\longrightarrow\;
    \log \;\longrightarrow\;
    \mathrm{DCT}\text{-II} \;\longrightarrow\;
    \mathbf{c}_t.
\end{equation*}
The power spectrum is summed through a bank of triangular filters spaced on
the mel scale (denser at low frequencies, where speech information lives),
the log of each band energy is taken, and an orthonormal DCT-II compresses
the 26 log-band energies down to the first 12 cepstral coefficients which
describe the smooth shape of the vocal-tract spectrum (as shown in Lecture 19). 
We additionally append the log frame energy and stack first- and second-order time
differences ($\Delta$ and $\Delta\Delta$) so each frame also encodes how
the spectrum is changing locally, giving a 39-D vector.
\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\linewidth]{../plots/mel_filterbank.png}
    \caption*{The 26-filter triangular mel filterbank applied to the power spectrum.}
\end{figure}

\emph{LPC (Linear Predictive Coding).} Each frame is modelled as the output
of an all-pole filter that captures the resonances of the vocal tract:
\begin{equation*}
    H(z) = \frac{G}{A(z)},
    \qquad
    A(z) = 1 + \sum_{k=1}^{p} a_k\, z^{-k}.
\end{equation*}
The coefficients $a_1,\dots,a_p$ are obtained from the frame's
autocorrelation via the Levinson--Durbin recursion. We use $p = 12$ and
append the log of the prediction-error energy as a loudness/voicing cue,
giving a 13-D vector.

\emph{LPCC (LP Cepstral Coefficients).} The cepstrum derived analytically
from the LPC coefficients we've obtained, with $\alpha_k = -a_k$:
\begin{equation*}
    c_m = \alpha_m + \sum_{k=1}^{m-1} \frac{k}{m}\, c_k\, \alpha_{m-k},
    \qquad 1 \le m \le p.
\end{equation*}
LPCCs retain qualities of the all-pole model with the better numerical
and statistical properties of cepstral features. It also deconvolves the 
vocal-tract response from the excitation, so it may be more robust to variations 
in pitch and preserve perceptual characteristics of speech (as in Lecture 16\&17). 
We keep $c_1,\dots,c_{12}$ and append first-order time differences, giving a 24-D vector.

\paragraph{(c) HMM classification.}
For each feature set we first fit a vector quantiser ($k$-means with
$V = 16$ centroids) on the stacked training frames and use it to encode
every utterance into a sequence of discrete symbols
$O = (o_1,\dots,o_T)$, $o_t \in \{1,\dots,V\}$. We then train one discrete
HMM per emotion class $c$, $\lambda_c = (\pi^c, A^c, B^c)$, with $K = 3$
hidden states using Baum--Welch (EM) algorithm (as in Lecture 19\&20). 
At test time the predicted emotion is the class whose model assigns the highest
likelihood to the symbol sequence:
\(
    \hat{y} = \arg\max_{c} \log P(O \mid \lambda_c).
\)


\subsection*{Results}

Test accuracies of our different feature sets are shown below (five classes,
chance $= 20\%$):

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
Feature set & Frame dim $D$ & Test accuracy \\
\midrule
LPC          & 13 & \textbf{0.367} \\
MFCC         & 39 & 0.356 \\
LPCC         & 24 & 0.278 \\
Combined     & 65 & 0.250 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

LPC and MFCC are essentially tied at the top; LPCC and the combined set
trail. The combined set's relative weakness is consistent with the
mismatch between its 65-D feature space and a 16-symbol codebook: too few
symbols to preserve class-discriminative structure in a much larger space.

\subsection*{Future Work}

This project utilizes the whole EC519 lecture pipeline and I think it is 
an interesting project since LLM (Whisper) have not gain the ability to 
understand the emotion of the speech. However, the results are not very good, 
and there is a lot of room for improvement.
Several directions follow naturally from the results above. One of the problems that
the combined feature set under-performs because a $V = 16$ codebook is too
coarse for a 65-D space; sweeping $V \in \{32, 64, 128\}$ might address this. 
Another aspect is that, throughout this project, I am intrigued by the potential of output vectorzied 
classification rather than probability like HMM does. Vectorized classification can be
modeled into 6 dimensional space for basic emotion (happy, sad, angry, fearful, suprise, disgust).
And that we can get rid of neutral, calm, and other non-basic emotion, which are more difficult to classify.
Third, our feature menu is purely spectral / source-filter;
adding \emph{prosodic} cues (pitch contour, energy envelope, speaking rate)
is well-motivated for emotion (e.g.\ anger and joy expand pitch range while
sadness compresses it). Finally, RAVDESS is
a small, acted, studio-clean dataset, so the absolute accuracies are likely
optimistic. Evaluating on a more naturalistic dataset such as
CREMA-D would test how much of the signal is genuine emotion.
